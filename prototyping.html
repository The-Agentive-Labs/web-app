Prototyping

Build interactive representations of your product to test ideas, gather feedback, and validate designs before development.

Learn

What is Prototyping?

Prototyping is the process of creating interactive, tangible representations of a product or feature before building the real thing. Prototypes range from paper sketches to high-fidelity interactive mockups. The goal: test assumptions, gather user feedback, and iterate quickly without the cost of full development.

Why Prototype?

  • Fail Fast, Fail Cheap: Discover flaws in paper or Figma, not in production code.
  • User Validation: Put something tangible in front of users to test behaviors, not hypotheticals.
  • Stakeholder Alignment: Show, don't tell. Prototypes communicate vision better than specs.
  • Design Iteration: Explore multiple solutions quickly. Prototypes are disposable—code isn't.

Fidelity Spectrum

  • Low-Fidelity: Paper sketches, wireframes, clickable wireframes. Fast to make, great for testing flows and concepts.
  • Mid-Fidelity: Grayscale mockups with basic interactions. Good for testing information architecture and hierarchy.
  • High-Fidelity: Pixel-perfect designs with full interactions, animations, and real content. Best for final validation before development.

Framework & Placement

Prototyping happens during Execution:

  • After: Problem definition, journey mapping, feature prioritization
  • Before: Development handoff and production build

Start with low-fidelity to test concepts, then increase fidelity as you validate assumptions.

Best Practices

  • Match Fidelity to Questions: Testing a concept? Use low-fi. Testing visual polish? Use high-fi. Don't over-invest early.
  • Make It Interactive: Static mockups can't test flows. Even simple clickable prototypes reveal usability issues.
  • Use Real Content: Lorem ipsum hides problems. Use realistic data, especially for complex interfaces.
  • Test Early and Often: Don't wait for perfection. Get a prototype in front of users within days, not weeks.
  • Focus on Critical Paths: Prototype the happy path and 1-2 edge cases. Don't build every screen.
  • Set Context: When testing, give users a scenario ("You just received an invoice..."). Don't explain the UI—see if they figure it out.

Build

Prototyping Tools

  • Paper & Pencil: Fastest for early concepts. Draw screens, cut them out, simulate clicks.
  • Figma: Industry standard for digital prototypes. Collaborative, component-based, supports interactions.
  • Framer: Code-based prototyping with advanced animations. Best for high-fidelity.
  • Marvel/InVision: Simple clickable prototypes from static images. Good for quick demos.
  • Code Prototypes: HTML/CSS/JS for testing technical feasibility or specific interactions.

Step-by-Step Instructions

  1. Define What You're Testing: What specific questions do you need answered? (e.g., "Can users complete onboarding in under 2 minutes?")
  2. Choose Fidelity Level: Low-fi for concepts and flows. High-fi for visual design and micro-interactions.
  3. Sketch the Flow: Map out the user journey on paper first. Identify critical screens and decision points.
  4. Build the Prototype: Create screens in your tool of choice. Add interactions (clicks, transitions). Focus on the critical path.
  5. Add Realistic Content: Replace placeholders with real or realistic data. Test with actual copy.
  6. Test with Users: Run 5-8 usability tests. Observe where users struggle. Don't explain—let them explore.
  7. Iterate: Fix major issues and test again. Repeat until the prototype validates your assumptions.

Example: Freelance Invoice App Prototype

Goal: Test if freelancers can create and send their first invoice in under 2 minutes.

Fidelity: Mid-fidelity clickable prototype in Figma.

Key Screens:

  • Onboarding: "Create your first invoice" prompt
  • Invoice form: Client name, amount, due date, description
  • Preview screen: Review before sending
  • Confirmation: "Invoice sent! You'll be notified when paid."

Interactions:

  • Click "Create Invoice" → Navigate to form
  • Fill fields → "Preview" button becomes active
  • Click "Send" → Show success animation + confirmation

Test Scenario: "You just finished a logo design for a client. Create an invoice for $500, due in 30 days, and send it."

Success Metrics: 90% of users complete task in under 2 minutes without asking for help.

AI + Human Collaboration

Human Value

  • Design intuition to craft intuitive, delightful user experiences
  • Contextual understanding of user needs and brand identity
  • Strategic judgment on what to prototype and test
  • Creative problem-solving for novel interaction patterns

AI Value

  • Generation of wireframe and layout variations
  • Conversion of sketches to digital mockups
  • Auto-generation of UI components from design systems
  • Rapid iteration of visual design alternatives

Key Considerations

  • AI for Speed, Not Strategy: AI can generate wireframe variations quickly, but humans must decide which direction aligns with user needs and brand.
  • Component Generation: AI can create UI components based on design systems, but humans must ensure consistency and usability.
  • Interaction Design Limits: AI struggles with complex interaction patterns and animations. Humans must define the "feel" of the prototype.

"AI in the Loop" Model

  1. Wireframe Generation: AI generates multiple layout options based on content hierarchy and best practices. Humans select and refine the most promising direction.
  2. Design System Application: AI applies design tokens (colors, typography, spacing) from your design system to components. Humans review for brand consistency.
  3. Content Population: AI generates realistic placeholder content (names, descriptions, data) for prototypes. Humans validate for relevance and tone.

Watch-Outs

  • Don't let AI design the entire prototype. It lacks the contextual understanding of user needs, brand voice, and strategic goals.
  • Avoid AI-generated "dark patterns." Always review AI suggestions for ethical design and accessibility.
  • Be cautious of AI overcomplicating simple flows. Humans must simplify and prioritize user needs.
  • AI-generated designs may lack originality. Use AI to accelerate iteration, but inject human creativity for differentiation.
