Synthesis Problem Framing

Framing Lenses

Reframe problems from multiple perspectives to uncover novel insights and breakthrough solutions.

Learn

What are Framing Lenses?

Framing Lenses are different perspectives you can use to view the same problem, revealing new insights and solution possibilities. The way you frame a problem determines the solutions you see. "How do we make invoicing faster?" yields different ideas than "How do we reduce freelancers' payment anxiety?" Lenses help you break out of default thinking patterns.

Why Reframe Problems?

  • Escape Fixation: First-instinct framings are often narrow. Reframing opens new solution spaces.
  • Challenge Assumptions: "The problem is X" may be wrong. Lenses force you to question your framing.
  • Unlock Creativity: Different lenses inspire different solutions. More framings = more creative options.
  • Align Teams: Teams often talk past each other because they're solving different problems. Explicit framing creates shared understanding.

Common Framing Lenses

  • User-Centered: "How might we help users accomplish [goal]?"
  • Pain Point: "How might we eliminate [frustration]?"
  • Emotional: "How might we make users feel [emotion]?"
  • Social: "How might we improve [interaction/relationship]?"
  • Technical: "How might we leverage [technology] to..."
  • Business: "How might we increase [metric/value]?"
  • Opposite: "What if we did the opposite of conventional wisdom?"
  • Analogous: "How do other industries solve similar problems?"

Best Practices

  • Generate Multiple Frames: Don't stop at one. Try 5-10 different framings of the same problem.
  • Use "How Might We" (HMW): This phrasing keeps framings open-ended and optimistic.
  • Challenge Your Default Frame: The first way you state a problem is rarely the best. Push yourself to reframe.
  • Mix Lenses: Combine user-centered + emotional, or technical + business. Compound framings reveal richer solutions.
  • Test with Solutions: Ideate briefly for each framing. Which frames inspire the best ideas?
  • Align on the Right Frame: Before building, ensure the team agrees on which framing to pursue.

Build

Template: Framing Exercise

Original Problem Statement:
[How you initially framed the problem]

Reframing #1 (User-Centered):
How might we help [user] accomplish [goal]?

Reframing #2 (Pain Point):
How might we eliminate [frustration]?

Reframing #3 (Emotional):
How might we make users feel [emotion]?

Reframing #4 (Social):
How might we improve [relationship/interaction]?

Reframing #5 (Opposite):
What if we [did the opposite]?

Reframing #6 (Analogous):
How do [other industry/domain] solve this?

Selected Frame: [Which framing unlocks the best solutions?]

Step-by-Step Instructions

  1. State Your Default Frame: Write down how you currently describe the problem. This is your baseline.
  2. Apply Lenses: Rewrite the problem using 5-7 different lenses (user, pain, emotional, social, technical, business, opposite, analogous).
  3. Brainstorm Solutions: For each framing, spend 5 minutes generating solution ideas. Don't judge—just generate.
  4. Compare Frames: Which framing inspired the most interesting/feasible/novel ideas?
  5. Synthesize: Can you combine multiple frames? (e.g., "How might we reduce payment anxiety [emotional] while saving time [pain]?")
  6. Choose & Commit: Select 1-2 framings to pursue. Share with your team for alignment.
  7. Test with Users: Validate that the framing resonates with real user needs.

Example: Freelance Invoice App

Original Frame: "How do we build an invoice tracking tool?"

Reframing #1 (User-Centered): "How might we help freelancers get paid faster and more reliably?"

Reframing #2 (Pain Point): "How might we eliminate the manual work of tracking invoice payments?"

Reframing #3 (Emotional): "How might we reduce freelancers' anxiety about cash flow?"

Reframing #4 (Social): "How might we make it less awkward for freelancers to follow up with clients on late payments?"

Reframing #5 (Technical): "How might we use automation (Stripe/PayPal APIs) to track payments in real-time?"

Reframing #6 (Opposite): "What if instead of tracking invoices, we ensured clients always pay on time? (Incentives? Contracts?)"

Reframing #7 (Analogous): "How do subscription services (Netflix, Spotify) handle payment tracking? Can we borrow their patterns?"

Insights:

  • Best Frame: #3 (Emotional) + #4 (Social). Reframing around anxiety and awkwardness reveals that automation alone isn't enough—users need reassurance and a way to follow up without feeling pushy.
  • Solution Implications: Build automated reminders (reduces awkwardness) + real-time status dashboard (reduces anxiety) + gentle nudge copy ("Friendly reminder from InvoiceFlow").

Selected Frame: "How might we reduce freelancers' payment anxiety and make follow-ups feel less awkward?"

AI + Human Collaboration

Human Value

  • Creative thinking to generate novel framings
  • Empathy to assess which frames resonate with users
  • Strategic judgment to select the most impactful framing
  • Contextual understanding to combine frames meaningfully

AI Value

  • Generation of multiple framing variations quickly
  • Suggestion of analogous problems from other domains
  • Identification of assumptions embedded in original framing
  • Cross-referencing with similar case studies and patterns

Key Considerations

  • AI for Quantity: Use AI to generate 10-20 reframings quickly. Humans select the most promising ones for deeper exploration.
  • Assumption Detection: AI can flag assumptions in your original framing (e.g., "assumes users want speed over accuracy"). Humans decide which assumptions to challenge.
  • Analogous Inspiration: AI can suggest how other industries solve similar problems. Humans adapt these analogies to your context.

"AI in the Loop" Model

  1. Initial Framing: Humans state the problem. AI flags embedded assumptions and suggests 5-10 alternative framings using different lenses.
  2. Analogous Thinking: AI proposes how other industries/products solve similar problems. Humans evaluate relevance and adapt patterns.
  3. Frame Selection: Humans brainstorm solutions for each framing. AI suggests which framings align best with user research and business goals. Humans make final call.

Watch-Outs

  • Don't let AI define the problem. Framing requires strategic judgment and deep user understanding that AI lacks.
  • Avoid generic AI-generated reframings. They may be grammatically correct but lack contextual depth.
  • Be cautious of AI analogies. Just because another industry does something doesn't mean it fits your users' needs.
  • Don't skip human ideation. AI can suggest frames, but humans must explore which frames unlock creative solutions.
