Discovery Desk Research

Secondary Research

Leverage existing data, reports, and studies to build foundational understanding before primary research.

Learn

What is Secondary Research?

Secondary Research (also called Desk Research) involves gathering and analyzing existing information—industry reports, academic studies, competitor analyses, market data—rather than collecting new data directly from users. It's the foundation layer that informs smarter primary research questions and prevents teams from "reinventing the wheel."

Why It Matters

Before spending time and money on interviews or surveys, secondary research helps you:

  • Understand the broader market landscape and trends
  • Identify what's already known (and what gaps exist)
  • Validate or challenge initial assumptions
  • Refine your problem hypothesis with evidence
  • Prepare smarter, more targeted primary research questions

Framework & Placement

Secondary Research happens early in the Discovery phase:

  • After: Problem hypothesis formulation
  • Before: Primary research (interviews, surveys, field studies)

It sets the stage for deeper, user-centered inquiry by establishing baseline knowledge.

Common Sources

  • Industry Reports: Gartner, Forrester, Nielsen, Statista
  • Academic Research: Google Scholar, ResearchGate, university libraries
  • Competitor Analysis: Product reviews (G2, Capterra), pricing pages, feature comparisons
  • Public Data: Census data, government databases (e.g., BLS, CDC), open datasets
  • Media & Trends: TechCrunch, HBR, trade publications, trend reports
  • Internal Data: Support tickets, CRM data, sales call notes, analytics

Best Practices

  • Start Broad, Then Narrow: Begin with general market trends, then zoom into specific user behaviors or pain points.
  • Evaluate Source Quality: Not all data is reliable. Check publication dates, sample sizes, and methodologies. Peer-reviewed > blog posts.
  • Document Everything: Keep a research log with links, key findings, and citations. You'll need to reference this later.
  • Look for Gaps: What questions can't be answered by secondary research? These become your primary research focus.
  • Synthesize, Don't Summarize: Don't just list findings. Connect dots, identify patterns, and form hypotheses.
  • Timebox It: Secondary research can be a rabbit hole. Set a deadline (e.g., 1-2 weeks) to prevent analysis paralysis.

Build

Template: Secondary Research Brief

Research Question: [What are you trying to learn?]

Sources Consulted:
- [Source 1: Title, Author, Year, Link]
- [Source 2: ...]

Key Findings:
1. [Finding 1: Summary + evidence]
2. [Finding 2: ...]

Patterns & Insights:
[Synthesize themes across sources. What's surprising? What's consistent?]

Gaps & Open Questions:
[What couldn't you answer? What needs primary research?]

Implications for Design:
[What does this mean for your product/strategy?]

Step-by-Step Instructions

  1. Define Your Research Question: What do you need to know? (e.g., "What are the top pain points for freelance designers managing invoices?")
  2. Identify Relevant Sources: List 5-10 high-quality sources (reports, studies, competitor reviews, forums). Prioritize recent data (last 2-3 years).
  3. Extract Key Data Points: As you read, highlight quantitative stats (e.g., "40% of freelancers report late payments") and qualitative insights (e.g., "Users describe invoice tracking as 'time-consuming'").
  4. Organize Findings by Theme: Group related data points. Use affinity mapping or a simple spreadsheet to cluster insights.
  5. Synthesize Patterns: What trends emerge? Are findings consistent across sources? Where do sources contradict?
  6. Identify Gaps: What questions remain unanswered? These gaps become your primary research focus.
  7. Document & Share: Write a 1-2 page brief summarizing findings, insights, and next steps. Share with your team.

Example: Freelance Invoice App

Research Question: What are the primary challenges freelancers face when managing invoices and payments?

Key Findings:

  • Finding 1: 43% of freelancers report late payments as their biggest financial stressor (Freelancers Union Survey, 2024).
  • Finding 2: Average time spent on invoicing: 2.5 hours/week (Freshbooks Report, 2023).
  • Finding 3: Top-rated features in invoice tools: automated reminders (87%), payment tracking (82%), mobile access (76%) (G2 Reviews).
  • Finding 4: Competitor weakness: QuickBooks rated "too complex for solo freelancers" (avg 2.8/5 on ease-of-use).

Patterns: Freelancers prioritize tools that save time and reduce payment anxiety. Simplicity > feature depth. Mobile access is increasingly critical (remote work trend).

Gaps: Why do freelancers tolerate manual processes (spreadsheets)? What psychological barriers prevent them from adopting invoice software?

Next Steps: Conduct 10-15 interviews with freelancers to validate findings and explore emotional motivations.

AI + Human Collaboration

Human Value

  • Critical thinking to evaluate source credibility and bias
  • Contextual judgment to assess relevance to your specific problem
  • Synthesis ability to connect disparate findings into coherent themes
  • Strategic prioritization of which gaps to explore in primary research

AI Value

  • Rapid scanning of large volumes of reports, articles, and studies
  • Extraction of key statistics and quotes from lengthy documents
  • Summarization of complex reports into digestible insights
  • Cross-referencing findings across multiple sources

Key Considerations

  • AI for Speed, Humans for Depth: AI can quickly surface relevant reports and summarize findings, but humans must assess quality, relevance, and strategic implications.
  • Source Validation: AI may pull from unreliable or outdated sources. Always verify publication dates, sample sizes, and methodologies manually.
  • Synthesis Over Summarization: AI excels at summarizing individual sources but struggles with cross-source synthesis. Humans must connect the dots and identify patterns.

"AI in the Loop" Model

  1. Source Discovery: AI scans databases (Google Scholar, industry reports) to identify relevant studies based on keywords. Humans evaluate and prioritize sources.
  2. Data Extraction: AI reads reports and extracts key data points (stats, quotes, methodologies). Humans review for accuracy and context.
  3. Pattern Identification: AI flags recurring themes across sources. Humans synthesize these themes, identifying insights and gaps that warrant primary research.

Watch-Outs

  • Don't rely on AI-generated summaries without reading the original source. AI may miss critical nuances or misinterpret findings.
  • Avoid "data dump" syndrome. Just because AI can surface 100 reports doesn't mean you should read them all. Focus on high-quality, relevant sources.
  • Be cautious of echo chambers. AI may surface sources that confirm your hypothesis rather than challenge it. Actively seek contradictory perspectives.
