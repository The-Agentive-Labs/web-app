SET Analysis

Analyze problems through Social, Emotional, and Technical lenses to ensure holistic solutions.

Learn

What is SET Analysis?

SET Analysis is a framework for examining user problems and design solutions across three dimensions: Social (how people interact), Emotional (how people feel), and Technical (how things work). It prevents teams from over-indexing on one dimension while neglecting others—ensuring solutions are technically feasible, emotionally resonant, and socially appropriate.

The Three Dimensions

  • Social: Group dynamics, cultural norms, collaboration, communication, status, identity, belonging
  • Emotional: Feelings, motivations, anxieties, desires, trust, confidence, satisfaction
  • Technical: Functionality, performance, reliability, usability, integration, scalability

Why SET Matters

Most teams default to technical solutions ("Let's build a feature!") while ignoring social and emotional needs. SET forces balanced thinking:

  • A technically perfect tool fails if it makes users feel anxious or incompetent
  • An emotionally delightful experience fails if it doesn't work reliably
  • A socially appropriate solution fails if it ignores how people actually collaborate

Framework & Placement

Use SET Analysis during Synthesis & Definition:

  • After: Primary research and affinity mapping
  • Before: Solution ideation and feature prioritization

It helps translate research findings into multi-dimensional problem statements.

Best Practices

  • Balance All Three: Don't let one dimension dominate. Technical feasibility matters, but so do emotions and social dynamics.
  • Root in Research: Each SET dimension should be supported by evidence (user quotes, observations, data).
  • Use for Problem AND Solution: Analyze the current problem through SET, then validate your solution addresses all three.
  • Collaborate Cross-Functionally: Engineers see Technical clearly. Designers see Emotional. Researchers see Social. Bring all perspectives.
  • Look for Conflicts: Sometimes dimensions contradict (e.g., technical efficiency vs. emotional reassurance). Identify tradeoffs explicitly.

Template: SET Analysis

Problem Being Analyzed: [Brief description]

Social Dimension
- How does this affect interactions between people?
- What social norms or dynamics are at play?
- Evidence: [User quotes, observations]

Emotional Dimension
- What feelings does this evoke? (anxiety, frustration, delight)
- What are the underlying motivations or fears?
- Evidence: [User quotes, behavioral signals]

Technical Dimension
- What are the functional requirements or constraints?
- What technical barriers exist?
- Evidence: [System limitations, performance data]

Insights & Tradeoffs
[Where do dimensions conflict? What must be balanced?]

Step-by-Step Instructions

  1. Define the Problem: State the user problem or design challenge you're analyzing.
  2. Analyze Social Factors: How does this problem affect relationships, collaboration, or group dynamics? What social context matters?
  3. Analyze Emotional Factors: What emotions do users experience? What are their motivations and anxieties?
  4. Analyze Technical Factors: What functional or system requirements exist? What are the constraints?
  5. Identify Conflicts: Where do dimensions contradict? (E.g., efficiency vs. emotional reassurance)
  6. Validate with Research: Ensure each dimension is supported by evidence, not assumptions.
  7. Design Holistically: Use SET to evaluate solution ideas. Does your solution address all three dimensions?

Example: Freelance Invoice App

Problem: Freelancers delay following up on overdue invoices, leading to cash flow problems.

Social Dimension:

  • Freelancers fear damaging client relationships by appearing "pushy" or "desperate"
  • Power dynamic: clients hold financial leverage, making confrontation risky
  • Evidence: "I don't want to seem like I'm nagging. What if they stop working with me?" (Interview #7)

Emotional Dimension:

  • Anxiety about cash flow and ability to pay bills
  • Embarrassment about asking for money ("feels awkward")
  • Desire to appear professional and in control
  • Evidence: "I lose sleep over unpaid invoices. But I hate being the person who has to ask." (Interview #3)

Technical Dimension:

  • No automated system to track invoice status or send reminders
  • Manual follow-up requires remembering due dates and composing emails
  • No integration between invoicing and payment platforms
  • Evidence: 85% of survey respondents use manual tracking (spreadsheets, email)

Solution Implications:

  • Social: Automate reminders so freelancers don't have to personally "nag." Frame as system-generated, not personal.
  • Emotional: Provide reassurance ("80% of invoices are paid within 7 days"). Reduce anxiety with visibility.
  • Technical: Build auto-reminders, payment tracking, and sync with Stripe/PayPal.

AI + Human Collaboration

Human Value

  • Empathy to interpret emotional and social nuances
  • Contextual understanding of cultural norms and power dynamics
  • Strategic judgment to balance conflicting dimensions
  • Ability to identify second-order effects of solutions

AI Value

  • Pattern recognition across research data (transcripts, surveys)
  • Tagging of emotional keywords and sentiment
  • Technical feasibility assessment based on system constraints
  • Cross-referencing SET dimensions with similar case studies

Key Considerations

  • AI for Data Tagging: Use AI to scan research data and tag content by SET category (social interactions, emotional language, technical mentions). Humans validate and interpret.
  • Emotional Analysis Limits: AI can flag emotional keywords ("anxious," "frustrated") but cannot understand the depth or cultural context. Humans must interpret.
  • Social Nuance: AI struggles with social dynamics, power structures, and cultural norms. These require human cultural intelligence.

"AI in the Loop" Model

  1. Data Categorization: AI scans research findings (interviews, surveys) and tags content by SET dimension. Humans review and refine categories.
  2. Pattern Identification: AI identifies recurring themes within each dimension (e.g., "payment anxiety" in Emotional). Humans assess strategic importance.
  3. Conflict Mapping: Humans identify where SET dimensions conflict. AI suggests similar cases or precedents. Humans decide on tradeoffs.

Watch-Outs

  • Don't let AI perform the entire SET analysis. It lacks the empathy, cultural awareness, and strategic judgment required.
  • Avoid over-reliance on AI sentiment analysis. Emotional and social dimensions require deep human interpretation.
  • Be cautious of AI missing power dynamics and social hierarchies. These are often implicit and require contextual understanding.
