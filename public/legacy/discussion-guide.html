Discovery Interview Planning

Discussion Guide

Structure interview sessions with strategic questions, prompts, and follow-ups to elicit rich user insights.

Learn

What is a Discussion Guide?

A Discussion Guide is a structured document that outlines the flow, questions, and prompts for user interviews or focus groups. It's not a rigid script—it's a flexible framework that ensures you cover key topics while leaving room for organic conversation. Think of it as your research GPS: it keeps you on track without forcing a specific route.

Why Use a Discussion Guide?

  • Consistency: Ensures all interviews cover the same topics, making synthesis easier.
  • Efficiency: Keeps interviews focused and on-time (30-60 mins).
  • Confidence: Reduces facilitator anxiety. You won't forget important questions.
  • Team Alignment: Multiple researchers can conduct interviews using the same structure.
  • Adaptability: Provides flexibility to probe deeper when interesting insights emerge.

Core Components

  • Introduction: Set expectations, build rapport, explain purpose
  • Warm-Up: Easy, non-threatening questions to ease participants in
  • Core Questions: The heart of the interview—exploring behaviors, problems, motivations
  • Probing Questions: Follow-ups to dig deeper ("Tell me more," "Why is that important?")
  • Wrap-Up: Final reflections, anything missed, next steps

Best Practices

  • Open-Ended Questions: "How do you..." beats "Do you...?" Stories reveal truth; yes/no answers don't.
  • Chronological Flow: Start broad, narrow down. Past behavior → Current state → Future needs.
  • Neutral Language: Avoid leading questions. "What do you think about X?" not "Don't you love X?"
  • Timebox Sections: Allocate time (e.g., Intro: 5min, Core: 40min, Wrap: 5min). Stay flexible but aware.
  • Build in Probes: Add follow-up prompts for common responses. Prepare for "I don't know" or silence.
  • Iterate: After 3-5 interviews, refine questions based on what's working (or not).

Build

Template: Discussion Guide

Research Goal: [What are we trying to learn?]
Duration: [45-60 minutes]

Introduction (5 min)
- Thank participant
- Explain purpose & confidentiality
- Get permission to record
- Set expectations (no right/wrong answers)

Warm-Up (5 min)
- Tell me about your [role/work/day]
- How long have you been [doing X]?

Core Questions (35-40 min)
Topic 1: [Behavioral Context]
- Walk me through the last time you [task]
- What prompted that? How did it go?
- [Probe: What made it easy/hard?]

Topic 2: [Pain Points]
- What's most frustrating about [process]?
- Describe a time when [problem] happened
- [Probe: How did you resolve it?]

Topic 3: [Motivations & Goals]
- Why is [goal] important to you?
- What would make [task] easier?

Wrap-Up (5 min)
- Anything we didn't cover?
- Who else should we talk to?
- Thank you & next steps

Step-by-Step Instructions

  1. Define Research Objectives: What specific questions do you need answered? Write them down.
  2. Identify Key Topics: Break objectives into 3-5 themes (e.g., current behaviors, pain points, needs).
  3. Write Questions: For each topic, write 2-4 open-ended questions. Focus on past behavior and stories.
  4. Add Probes: Under each question, add 2-3 follow-up prompts to dig deeper.
  5. Time It: Assign time to each section. Total should be 45-60 mins.
  6. Pilot the Guide: Test with 1-2 people. Are questions clear? Do you get useful answers? Adjust as needed.
  7. Use It Flexibly: During interviews, follow the guide but allow organic detours. If a participant says something interesting, probe deeper.

Example: Freelance Invoice App Discussion Guide

Research Goal: Understand freelancers' invoicing workflows, pain points, and unmet needs.

Introduction (5 min): "Thanks for joining. We're learning about how freelancers manage invoices and payments. No right or wrong answers—just your honest experience. Can I record for notes?"

Warm-Up (5 min):

  • "Tell me about your freelance work. What do you do?"
  • "How many clients do you work with at a time?"

Core Questions (40 min):

Topic 1: Current Process

  • "Walk me through the last time you invoiced a client. What steps did you take?"
  • [Probe: What tools did you use? How long did it take?]
  • "How do you track whether clients have paid?"
  • [Probe: Where do you keep that information? How often do you check?]

Topic 2: Pain Points

  • "What's the most frustrating part of invoicing and tracking payments?"
  • "Tell me about a time when an invoice went unpaid. What happened?"
  • [Probe: How did you follow up? How did it make you feel?]

Topic 3: Needs & Solutions

  • "If you had a magic wand, what would you change about invoicing?"
  • "What would make you feel more in control of getting paid?"

Wrap-Up (5 min): "Is there anything I didn't ask that I should have?" "Thanks! We'll share findings soon."

AI + Human Collaboration

Human Value

  • Empathy to craft questions that resonate with participants
  • Contextual understanding to sequence topics logically
  • Strategic judgment to prioritize which topics matter most
  • Adaptability to refine guide based on pilot learnings

AI Value

  • Suggestion of question formats and phrasing
  • Generation of follow-up probes for common responses
  • Benchmarking against research best practices
  • Identification of leading or biased language

Key Considerations

  • AI for Question Drafting: Use AI to generate initial question ideas. But humans must refine for context, tone, and strategic focus.
  • Bias Detection: AI can flag leading questions ("Don't you think...?"). But humans must ensure neutrality throughout.
  • Cultural Sensitivity: AI may miss cultural nuances in question phrasing. Humans must adapt for audience.

"AI in the Loop" Model

  1. Drafting: Humans define research objectives. AI suggests question frameworks and phrasing. Humans refine for clarity and context.
  2. Probing: AI generates follow-up probe ideas for each question. Humans select the most useful probes and add custom ones.
  3. Iteration: After pilot interviews, humans identify weak questions. AI suggests alternatives. Humans test and finalize.

Watch-Outs

  • Don't let AI write the entire guide. It lacks strategic context and understanding of your specific research goals.
  • Avoid AI-generated generic questions. They work for any topic but lack depth. Tailor questions to your product and users.
  • Be cautious of AI-suggested "best practices" that don't fit your context. Not every guide needs the same structure.
  • Never use AI to conduct interviews. Discussion guides are for human facilitators only.
