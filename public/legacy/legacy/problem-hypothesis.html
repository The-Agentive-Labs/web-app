Discovery Problem Framing

Problem Hypothesis

Articulate a clear, testable assumption about a user problem to guide early research efforts.

Learn

What is a Problem Hypothesis?

A Problem Hypothesis is an educated guess about a challenge or unmet need your users may face. It acts as a North Star for early discovery research, helping teams focus their efforts before they have validated insights. Think of it as planting a flag in uncharted territory—you believe there's something valuable there, but you need to explore to confirm it.

Framework & Placement

Problem Hypotheses are formulated at the very start of the product discovery process—even before primary research. They emerge from:

  • Observed market trends or competitive gaps
  • Anecdotal feedback from support tickets or sales calls
  • Visionary product ideas or strategic business goals
  • Light secondary research (articles, industry reports, internal data)

Once stated, the hypothesis becomes the focus of exploratory research (interviews, surveys, secondary research) to validate or invalidate the assumption.

Best Practices

  • Be Specific: Vague problems lead to vague research. "Users struggle with productivity" is too broad. "Freelance designers waste time re-invoicing clients due to unclear payment terms" is actionable.
  • Make it Testable: Frame your hypothesis so you can prove or disprove it through research. Avoid opinions; focus on observable behaviors or outcomes.
  • Stay User-Centered: Ground the hypothesis in a user need, not just a business goal. "We need to increase revenue" is not a problem hypothesis. "SMB owners lack affordable tools to manage inventory" is.
  • Embrace Being Wrong: A disproven hypothesis is still valuable—it saves you from building the wrong thing. Research is about learning, not confirming your biases.
  • Limit Scope: Start with one clear problem. You can expand later, but early focus prevents analysis paralysis.

Build

Template: Problem Hypothesis Statement

We believe that [USER SEGMENT]
experiences [PROBLEM/PAIN POINT]
when [SITUATION/CONTEXT].

This matters because [IMPACT/CONSEQUENCE].

We will know we're right if we observe [SUCCESS CRITERIA/EVIDENCE].

Step-by-Step Instructions

  1. Identify the User Segment: Who are you designing for? Be specific (e.g., "remote workers in tech startups" vs. "office workers").
  2. Define the Problem: What pain point or unmet need do you suspect exists? Use clear, observable language.
  3. Add Context: When or where does this problem occur? Context helps narrow the scope and makes the problem more tangible.
  4. Articulate the Impact: Why does this problem matter? What are the consequences if it goes unsolved?
  5. Set Success Criteria: What evidence would validate your hypothesis? This could be user quotes, behavioral data, or measurable outcomes.
  6. Review & Refine: Share your hypothesis with stakeholders. Does it align with business goals? Is it specific enough to guide research?

Example

Hypothesis:

We believe that freelance graphic designers experience cash flow anxiety and wasted administrative time when tracking payments from multiple clients with inconsistent invoicing schedules.

This matters because missed or delayed payments can threaten their financial stability, and time spent chasing invoices reduces billable hours.

We will know we're right if we observe designers mentioning invoice tracking as a top-3 pain point in interviews, or if we find data showing 40%+ of invoices are paid late.

AI + Human Collaboration

Human Value

  • Intuition from lived experience and domain expertise
  • Ability to read between the lines and spot nuanced patterns
  • Contextual judgment to assess what's strategically important
  • Empathy to frame problems in human-centered terms

AI Value

  • Rapid synthesis of large datasets (competitor info, industry reports, forums)
  • Identification of language patterns in user feedback (support tickets, reviews)
  • Generation of multiple hypothesis variations for team discussion
  • Benchmarking against similar problems in other industries

Key Considerations

  • AI as a Research Assistant: Use AI to scan Reddit threads, reviews, or support logs for recurring themes. But always validate with real user conversations—AI can spot frequency, not emotional weight.
  • Bias Risk: AI models reflect the data they're trained on. If you ask AI to generate a hypothesis, it may default to common assumptions rather than novel insights. Always stress-test AI-generated ideas with human intuition.
  • Hypothesis Refinement: AI excels at rewriting and sharpening language. Use it to make your hypothesis more concise or testable, but ensure the core idea stays human-driven.

"AI in the Loop" Model

Three ways AI can enhance problem hypothesis development:

  1. Exploratory Input: AI scans secondary sources (articles, forums, competitor reviews) to surface potential problem areas. Humans then curate and prioritize based on strategic fit.
  2. Iterative Shaping: Humans draft an initial hypothesis. AI suggests alternative framings, identifies ambiguities, or proposes testable success criteria. The team refines collaboratively.
  3. Validation Prep: AI generates interview questions or survey prompts aligned with the hypothesis. Humans review for tone, bias, and cultural appropriateness before deployment.

Watch-Outs

  • Don't let AI generate the entire hypothesis from scratch—it lacks strategic context and user empathy.
  • Avoid over-reliance on AI-scraped data (reviews, forums) as the sole foundation. This data is noisy and may not represent your actual user base.
  • Be cautious of "HiPPO Bias" (Highest Paid Person's Opinion). AI can accidentally amplify existing biases if trained on your internal documents. Seek external perspectives.
