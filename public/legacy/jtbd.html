Jobs to Be Done (JTBD)

Frame user needs as functional goals ("jobs") rather than demographics or features.

Learn

What is Jobs to Be Done?

Jobs to Be Done (JTBD) is a framework that shifts focus from who users are to what they're trying to accomplish. The core idea: people "hire" products to do a job. Understanding the job—not the demographics—reveals why users choose your product over alternatives.

Example: A milkshake isn't hired for its flavor, but for the job of "keeping me full during my commute." This lens helps teams innovate beyond surface-level features.

Framework & Placement

JTBD is used during Strategy & Planning:

  • After: User interviews, personas, and journey mapping
  • Before: Feature prioritization, roadmap planning, and solution design

It bridges user research and product strategy by translating behaviors into actionable product requirements.

Core Concepts

  • Functional Job: The core task (e.g., "send money to a friend quickly")
  • Emotional Job: How users want to feel (e.g., "feel confident I'm managing finances responsibly")
  • Social Job: How users want to be perceived (e.g., "appear generous to friends")
  • Circumstances: The context that triggers the job (e.g., "splitting a dinner bill")
  • Success Criteria: What "done" looks like (e.g., "payment received instantly, no awkward follow-up")

Best Practices

  • Start with "When... I want to...": Frame jobs as situational needs, not product features.
  • Focus on Progress, Not Tasks: Users don't want a drill; they want a hole in the wall. What progress are they trying to make?
  • Include Emotional and Social Dimensions: Functional jobs alone miss why users care. Dig into feelings and identity.
  • Map Competing Solutions: What do users hire today? (Competitors, workarounds, DIY hacks?) This reveals unmet needs.
  • Validate with Real Stories: Ask users to describe a recent time they "hired" a solution. Stories reveal true jobs, not hypothetical ones.

Build

Template: JTBD Statement

When [SITUATION],
I want to [MOTIVATION/GOAL],
So I can [EXPECTED OUTCOME].

Functional Job: [Core task to accomplish]
Emotional Job: [Desired feeling/state]
Social Job: [Desired perception by others]

Step-by-Step Instructions

  1. Identify the Situation: What triggers the need? Be specific about context (time, place, circumstances).
  2. Define the Motivation: What is the user trying to achieve? Focus on the goal, not the product.
  3. Articulate the Outcome: What does success look like? What changes when the job is done?
  4. Layer in Emotional/Social Jobs: What feelings or social signals matter? How does this job relate to the user's identity?
  5. Map Current Solutions: What do users hire today? (Your product, competitors, workarounds?) Why do they switch or stick?
  6. Identify Underserved Jobs: Where are existing solutions failing? What progress do users struggle to make?
  7. Validate with User Stories: Interview 5-10 users. Ask them to recount a specific time they faced this situation. Do their stories confirm the job?

Example: Freelance Invoice App

JTBD Statement:

When I complete a project for a client,
I want to get paid quickly and without awkward follow-up,
So I can maintain cash flow and focus on my next project.

Functional Job: Create and send professional invoices; track payment status; automate reminders.

Emotional Job: Feel confident and in control of finances; reduce anxiety about late payments.

Social Job: Appear professional and organized to clients; avoid seeming desperate when chasing payments.

Current Solutions (Competing Products):

  • Google Sheets + Gmail (free, but manual and error-prone)
  • QuickBooks (powerful, but overkill for freelancers—too complex)
  • PayPal Invoicing (integrated payments, but limited customization)

Underserved Need: Existing tools are either too manual (spreadsheets) or too complex (enterprise software). Freelancers need a middle ground: simple, automated, and payment-integrated.

AI + Human Collaboration

Human Value

  • Empathy to uncover emotional and social job dimensions
  • Contextual understanding of situational triggers
  • Strategic judgment to prioritize high-impact jobs
  • Ability to probe beyond surface-level responses in interviews

AI Value

  • Pattern recognition across large interview datasets
  • Identification of recurring "when... I want to..." phrases
  • Mapping of competing solutions from market research
  • Generation of JTBD statement variations for refinement

Key Considerations

  • AI for Job Extraction: Use AI to scan interview transcripts for language patterns ("I need to," "I wish I could," "I struggle with"). But humans must interpret context—AI misses sarcasm and nuance.
  • Emotional Layer: AI can flag emotional keywords ("anxious," "frustrated," "confident"), but humans must validate the emotional job through follow-up questions.
  • Job Prioritization: AI can quantify job frequency (how often it's mentioned), but humans must assess strategic importance (impact, differentiation, feasibility).

"AI in the Loop" Model

  1. Job Hypothesis Generation: AI scans user feedback (interviews, support tickets, reviews) to propose potential jobs. Humans validate and refine based on strategic fit.
  2. Competing Solution Mapping: AI analyzes competitor reviews and market data to identify what users currently "hire." Humans assess why users switch or churn.
  3. JTBD Statement Drafting: AI generates initial "When... I want to..." statements. Humans refine for clarity, add emotional/social dimensions, and validate with users.

Watch-Outs

  • Don't let AI define jobs based on product features. JTBD is about user progress, not "use our calendar tool." Keep it outcome-focused.
  • Avoid over-reliance on AI sentiment analysis. Emotional jobs require deep listening—AI can't replace human empathy in interviews.
  • Be cautious of "average job" bias. AI may surface the most common job, missing high-value niche jobs that differentiate your product.
