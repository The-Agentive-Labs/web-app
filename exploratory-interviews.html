Discovery Qualitative Research

Exploratory Interviews

Conduct open-ended conversations with users to uncover problems, motivations, and behaviors.

Learn

What are Exploratory Interviews?

Exploratory Interviews are semi-structured, open-ended conversations designed to understand user behaviors, needs, and pain points before you've committed to a solution. Unlike usability testing (which validates designs), exploratory interviews help you discover the right problems to solve.

When to Use

Conduct exploratory interviews during the Discovery phase:

  • When you have a problem hypothesis but need to validate it
  • Before designing solutions (don't show mockups—just listen)
  • When entering a new market or user segment
  • To understand the "why" behind quantitative data

Core Principles

  • Ask "Why" 5 Times: Surface-level answers hide deeper motivations. Keep probing.
  • Focus on Past Behavior: "Tell me about the last time you..." beats "Would you ever...?" Stories reveal truth; hypotheticals don't.
  • Stay Neutral: Don't lead with your solution or bias responses. You're here to learn, not validate.
  • Embrace Silence: Pause after the user speaks. Silence invites them to elaborate.
  • Observe Non-Verbals: Tone, hesitation, and body language reveal as much as words.

Best Practices

  • Sample Size: 10-15 interviews typically reveal 80%+ of themes. Diminishing returns after that.
  • Duration: 30-60 minutes per interview. Shorter = surface-level; longer = fatigue.
  • Record & Transcribe: Focus on listening, not note-taking. Get permission to record.
  • Two-Person Teams: One facilitates, one takes notes. Both debrief immediately after.
  • Iterate Your Guide: After 3-5 interviews, refine questions based on what you're learning.
  • Compensate Participants: Respect their time. Gift cards or cash are standard ($50-150 depending on audience).

Build

Template: Interview Guide

Introduction (5 min)
- Thank you for your time
- Purpose: Learn about your experience with [topic]
- No right/wrong answers; we want honest feedback
- Recording for notes (with permission)
- Confidentiality: We won't share your name

Warm-Up (5 min)
- Tell me about your role/work
- Walk me through a typical day

Core Questions (35-45 min)
1. Tell me about the last time you [relevant activity]
- What prompted that?
- How did you approach it?
- What went well? What was frustrating?

2. What tools/methods do you currently use for [task]?
- Why those specifically?
- What do you like/dislike?

3. Describe a time when [problem scenario]
- What caused that?
- How did you resolve it?
- What would have made it easier?

Wrap-Up (5 min)
- Is there anything I didn't ask that I should have?
- Who else should I talk to?
- Thank you + next steps

Step-by-Step Instructions

  1. Define Your Research Questions: What do you need to learn? (Not "Will users like our idea?" but "What problems do users face with X?")
  2. Recruit Participants: Target your user segment. Use screeners to filter. Recruit 15-20 to account for no-shows.
  3. Craft Your Interview Guide: Write 5-10 open-ended questions. Focus on stories, not opinions.
  4. Pilot the Guide: Test with 1-2 people. Time it. Refine confusing questions.
  5. Conduct Interviews: Create a comfortable environment. Start broad, then narrow. Probe deeply when something interesting emerges.
  6. Debrief Immediately: Spend 10 minutes after each interview capturing key insights while they're fresh.
  7. Synthesize Findings: Use affinity mapping to cluster themes across interviews. Look for patterns, surprises, and contradictions.

Example Questions: Freelance Invoice App

Warm-Up: "Tell me about your freelance work. How many clients do you typically work with at a time?"

Core: "Walk me through the last time you invoiced a client. What steps did you take? Which parts felt smooth, and which felt tedious?"

Probing: "You mentioned tracking payments is stressful—can you tell me more about a specific time that happened? What made it stressful?"

Pain Point Exploration: "What tools do you use to manage invoices today? Why those? What do they do well? Where do they fall short?"

Emotional Layer: "How do you feel when an invoice is overdue? What goes through your mind when deciding whether to follow up?"

Wrap-Up: "If you had a magic wand and could fix one thing about invoicing, what would it be?"

AI + Human Collaboration

Human Value

  • Empathy to build rapport and make participants comfortable
  • Active listening to pick up on emotional cues and body language
  • Adaptability to probe deeper when something interesting emerges
  • Contextual judgment to distinguish signal from noise

AI Value

  • Transcription of interview recordings with timestamps
  • Extraction of key quotes and themes from transcripts
  • Pattern identification across multiple interviews
  • Suggested follow-up questions based on responses

Key Considerations

  • AI for Transcription: Use AI to transcribe interviews automatically, freeing you to focus on listening. But always review transcripts—AI misses sarcasm, tone, and overlapping speech.
  • Quote Mining: AI can scan transcripts to extract impactful quotes related to specific themes. But humans must assess which quotes are representative vs. outliers.
  • No AI in the Room: Don't use AI during live interviews. It introduces privacy concerns and can make participants uncomfortable. Focus on human connection.

"AI in the Loop" Model

  1. Preparation: AI suggests interview questions based on research objectives and industry best practices. Humans refine questions for specificity and cultural appropriateness.
  2. Post-Interview Processing: AI transcribes recordings and tags key moments (pain points, workarounds, emotional reactions). Humans review and annotate with context.
  3. Synthesis: AI clusters quotes by theme across interviews. Humans validate clusters, identify patterns, and prioritize insights for action.

Watch-Outs

  • Never let AI conduct the interview. User research requires empathy, adaptability, and human intuition that AI cannot replicate.
  • Don't rely solely on AI-generated questions. They're generic and miss your product's unique context. Use AI as a starting point, not the final guide.
  • Be cautious of AI sentiment analysis. It can misread tone, sarcasm, and cultural nuances. Always validate with human review.
  • Privacy first: If using AI transcription, ensure compliance with privacy laws (GDPR, etc.) and inform participants.
